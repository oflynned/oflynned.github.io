ENVIRONMENTAL CONCERN

The CO2 Problem

Greenhouse gases trap heat from the sun and increase the temperature of our planet. Carbon emissions, resulting from the combustion of fossil fuels, are the largest contributors to greenhouse gases in our atmosphere. The use of energy in our homes, businesses and public work places all contribute to this problem, rapidly bolstering concentrations of atmospheric CO2. This ongoing trend is cause for concern because it is leading to adverse environmental changes which will affect us all.

Forestry and the Carbon Cycle

Forests play a unique role in the Earth’s carbon cycle. Trees absorb CO2 by means of photosynthesis and release only oxygen back into the atmosphere while the remaining carbon is stored in trees’ hard structures such as roots, trunks and branches. These structures have the potential to lock-up large amounts of carbon. In addition, carbon rich detritus such as leaves, twigs and bark, which fall from the trees are continuously worked into and stored within soils. Today, as such, forests are the biggest terrestrial sinks of carbon, absorbing an estimated 20% of man’s emissions. This helps to maintain environmental stability and a favourable climate on Earth.

CARBON CALCULATIONS

How We Calculate

To ensure that our predictions of CO2 yield are as true to life as they can be, as our trees grow, we regularly take measurements from a sub-sample of each of our tree species. We take simple measurements of tree height, trunk diameter and the area covered by roots and branches. This allows us to accurately assess how well our trees are growing and the weight of CO2 which is being sequestered. This data allows PlusPlant to validate and constantly refine predictions of CO2 yields and adjust the amount of forestry needed to offset CO2 respectively.

CO2 in Trees

Calculating CO2 is not an easy task. Carbon is an essential element for the growth of a tree but… how do you measure its weight? Ironically PlusPlant borrows data from the logging industry, which provides us with the most accurate predictions of the total biomass yield we can expect when planting each of our tree species. Then using current ecological data, we can estimate the weight of CO2 (in kg) relative to the total biomass yield.

CO2 in Soils

Equally challenging is the estimation of the CO2 which is stored in soils. When tree litter falls and begins to decompose microbiologically, a protein called glomalin is formed. Glomalin acts to aggregate carbon rich matter with silts, sands and clays turning it into a more solid substance, known as humus. Humus and CO2 are incrementally worked into soil as more robust matter. Accurately measuring the “brown CO2” stored in our soils involves regularly measuring, weighing and chemically analysing the incremental build-up of humus at our site.
